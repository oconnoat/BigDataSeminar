The standard approach to describing Big Data is that it is data that can't be analysed using conventional Business Intelligence methods. The origins of the term are highly industrial. As a marketing term, and especially given the rate of technology and hype, it's a pretty vague term. The important thing I want to convey is what the approach means, what the technologies are and how it might be useful to humanities work.

The first step is to collect the data: from twitter, from a dusty archive in darkest Offaly, anywhere.
The next thing is to create the digital incarnation of the collection: photographs, transcriptions, points on a map. The data needs to be made consistent so that it can be used. Internal consistency is important, but also with reference to other collections. Enrichment can be manual, automatic or both, and might include highlighting entities (people, places, times, dates, etc) or linking to other databases. The licensing is not trivial, but is a key component of what's then offfered.

The software is almost never the problem: you need to have a clear understanding of what you want to do and how you want to achieve it. You probably do need a programmer, or a technical person, but it's important to realise that maintenance is often far harder than deployment.
mapreduce kicked off the big data revolution because it was the technique that made truly massive quantities of truly diverse data accessible. To be honest, I doubt most projects will need to use industrial-scale tools like this. If you do, you probably also need a team of professionals.
